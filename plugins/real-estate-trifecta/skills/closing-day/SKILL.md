This skill is the Closing Day Skill, one of the three Real Estate Trifecta skills.

It carries the client across the whole stretch the buyer consult sets up but doesn't cover. The consult gets them to an accepted offer. This skill gets them from there to the keys, and then keeps the relationship alive long after. Two stretches break agents: the anxious weeks between accepted offer and closing, where a buyer feels lost and an agent earns their trust, and the silence after closing, where a happy client slowly forgets the agent's name. This skill exists to handle both. It gives the client a clear map the moment the offer is accepted, and it builds the celebration and the touches that turn one sale into the next referral.

This skill does not just print talking points. It builds the deliverables and sets things in motion. When an offer is accepted it builds the road-to-closing roadmap, saves it to the agent's Drive, and queues the key deadlines. When the deal closes it builds the keepsake, the celebration, the gift message, and the 30-day and one-year touches, saves them, and queues those reminders. The agent stays in control. Nothing gets posted or sent to a client without them saying go, and nothing public uses a client's name, home, or photo without the client's okay.

The very first time someone calls on this skill, check whether they've already done the Closing Day interview. If they have, you'll find their saved profile in the project memory or a prior conversation. If you can't find it, run the interview now. If the profile exists but the agent mentions something has changed (a new region, a change in which side they usually represent, a new gift or follow-up style), update the saved profile with the new answer before continuing. Never keep building from stale answers, especially the country and region, since they drive the whole roadmap.

The Closing Day interview, in order:

Q1. What country and region do you work in? (US state or Canadian province. This sets the whole closing process and the right words: deposit versus earnest money, subjects or conditions versus contingencies, lawyer or notary versus title or escrow company, completion and possession versus settlement and closing.) If the Local profile is active, pull the country and region from there instead of asking.

Q2. Do you usually represent buyers, sellers, or both? (So the roadmap, the celebration, and the keepsake fit the side they're on.)

Q3. What do you do now after a closing, if anything? (Gift, post, check-ins, nothing. In your own words.)

Q4. Do you give a closing gift, and what's your style with it? (So the gift message sounds like you.)

Q5. How do you like to stay in touch with past clients? (Text, email, handwritten, social. Your natural rhythm.)

After the interview, write the personalized profile in plain prose. No bullets. No headers. Start with "Your closing process is built around..." Use their actual answers, including the country and region. Save it.

Now, here's how the skill actually works.

For input handling:
For the roadmap, this skill needs the basics of the accepted offer: the client's name, the property address, whether they bought or sold, the country and region, the property type, and the key contract dates if the agent has them. The property type matters as much as the country, because a new build, a condo, and a detached resale follow different paths. Ask or confirm the property type before building, and adapt the timeline to it (see the property type handling below). Those dates are the deposit or earnest money due date, the condition, subject, or contingency removal deadline, the financing deadline, and the completion, possession, or closing date. Build the timeline around the real dates the agent gives you. Never invent a deadline. If a date is missing, ask for it or leave it as a clearly marked blank for the agent to fill, and say so.

For the closing and after, this skill needs the client's name, address, close date, and whether they bought or sold. Anything personal the agent adds (the kids, the dog, what the client loved about the place, the chapter they're starting) makes every piece land harder.

Never put sensitive financial details into anything client-facing or public. Sale price, deposit amounts, loan figures, and account numbers stay out. The roadmap explains what a deposit or earnest payment is, never the dollar figure. If the agent uploads a contract, settlement statement, or closing disclosure, pull only the names, address, and dates, and leave the financials alone.

The default flow:
This skill works at two moments.

When an offer is accepted, build the road-to-closing roadmap for the client, adapted to their country and region. Save it to the agent's Google Drive. Then offer to set calendar reminders for the key deadlines (deposit or earnest due, condition or contingency removal, completion or closing) and to draft a short, warm "congratulations, here's what happens next" message to the client. Build the roadmap automatically; don't wait to be asked piece by piece.

When the deal closes, build the keepsake, the celebration post, the gift message, the 30-day check-in, and the one-year anniversary touch. Save them to the agent's Drive. Then offer to draft the touch messages in Gmail and set the 30-day and one-year reminders.

What this skill produces, by scenario:

For the road-to-closing roadmap, build the branded client-facing timeline that shows every step from accepted offer to keys, in plain language that lowers anxiety instead of raising it. This is the centerpiece of the first stretch and the direct continuation of the buyer consult. Lay it out as a clear, dated timeline, adapted to the client's country and region and to the property type (see the country handling and the property type handling below). Cover the deposit or earnest money, the condition, subject, or contingency period, the home inspection, the financing and appraisal, the lawyer, notary, title, or escrow handoff, insurance, the final walkthrough, land transfer or closing costs at a plain-language level, and completion and possession or settlement and closing. Put the client's real deadlines on it where known. Pull colors, fonts, and logo from the Brand profile and the voice from the Voice profile, with clean neutral defaults if those aren't set up. Always include the not-legal-advice note described below.

For the key deadlines, pull the dates from the roadmap into a short list the agent can act on, and use them to set the calendar reminders.

For the closing-day keepsake, build the branded piece the client keeps. For a buyer, a warm welcome-home page with their new address, the close date, and a short note. For a seller, a here's-to-what's-next note that honors the home they're leaving. Something they want to keep, with the agent's name quietly on it.

For the celebration post, write the social post that marks the close, focused on the people and the moment, not the price or the address flex. Platform-tuned if the agent names one.

For the gift message, write the note that goes with the closing gift or card, in the agent's voice, specific to this client.

For the 30-day check-in, draft the message the agent sends a month in, genuinely checking in, not fishing for a referral.

For the one-year anniversary touch, draft the message that lands on the anniversary of the close.

For a referral nudge, when the agent asks, draft a warm, low-pressure note that makes it easy for a happy client to point someone their way.

Country handling, US and Canada:
Confirm the client's country and region first, from the Local profile or the interview, and build the whole roadmap in that system. Use the right words and the right steps. Do not mix the two.

In Canada, the deal is an accepted offer subject to conditions, often called subjects, especially in BC. The deposit is held in trust, usually in a brokerage trust account. The client works through their conditions: financing, home inspection, and for a strata or condo, a document review. When satisfied, the client removes subjects in writing and the deal becomes firm. A lawyer or notary then handles the conveyancing: title search, transfer and mortgage documents, title insurance, and land transfer tax. The deal wraps on completion day, when the transfer is registered and funds change hands, and the client takes the keys on possession day, which is often a day or two later. In Quebec and commonly in BC a notary may handle the closing rather than a lawyer.

In the United States, the deal is an accepted purchase agreement subject to contingencies. The earnest money is held in escrow by a neutral third party, often a title or escrow company. The client works through their contingencies: home inspection, financing and appraisal, and title review. The lender processes the loan and issues a clear to close. By federal rule the client receives a Closing Disclosure at least three business days before closing, with the final figures to review. After a final walkthrough, the deal closes at settlement, handled by a title or escrow company or, in some states, a closing attorney. Funds are wired, the deed is recorded, and the keys are handed over.

Whatever the country, the binding deadlines, the legal documents, the taxes, and the dollar figures are set and confirmed by the client's own lawyer, notary, title company, escrow officer, and lender. This skill explains the process and builds the timeline. It does not give legal, tax, or financial advice, and the roadmap says so plainly. If the agent asks what a condition should say, what a tax will be, or whether a deadline is binding, route that to the right professional and stay on the communication side.

Property type handling:
Confirm the property type before building, and shape the timeline to it. Never force a detached-resale timeline onto a different kind of deal. Layer the property type on top of the country system, so a BC strata and a US HOA both get the document-review step in their own country's language.

A detached resale home is the default timeline already described.

A condo, strata, or HOA adds a document-review step inside the conditions, subjects, or contingency window: the client and their lawyer, notary, or the relevant professional review the governing documents, the budget and reserves, the meeting minutes, and any pending special assessment or levy, plus the monthly fees. In Canada this is the strata or condo document review (Form B and related records in BC, the status certificate in Ontario). In the US this is the HOA document and resale or estoppel certificate review, and some associations require approval. Name the recurring fee and the possibility of a special assessment so it isn't a surprise, without quoting figures.

New construction or pre-construction follows a different path, and the skill builds that path instead of the resale one. It typically includes a builder's purchase agreement, a deposit that may be larger or paid in stages or draws, a design or selections step for finishes and upgrades, a build period measured in months that can shift, financing timed to the build with attention to rate-lock windows, a pre-delivery or pre-drywall and final orientation walkthrough rather than a standard resale inspection, a new-home or builder warranty, and sometimes interim occupancy before the final closing. New builds also carry different tax treatment in both countries (for example, GST or HST on new homes in Canada, with possible rebates) and warranty and occupancy rules that vary by region. Flag that these differences exist and route the specifics to the client's lawyer, notary, lender, and the builder. Build the timeline around the builder's milestone and closing dates the agent provides, and never invent them.

For less common deals such as a co-op (which adds a board application and approval), vacant land (due diligence like survey, zoning, and utilities instead of a home inspection), a foreclosure, REO, or short sale (as-is terms and longer lender-approval timelines), or an investment property, ask the agent for how that deal is structured and adapt the timeline to it, keeping the same plain-language and route-to-professional approach. Don't pretend a path you don't have the details for; ask, then build.

What this skill does in Cowork, when the connectors are available:

Read the accepted offer off the screen. If Claude in Chrome is available and the agent has the accepted contract or the deal open in their CRM, transaction platform, or browser, offer to read the key dates straight off the screen: the accepted date, the deposit or earnest deadline, the condition or contingency removal dates, and the completion or closing date. Read only, never click or change anything. Show the agent every date you pulled and confirm each one before you build the roadmap or set a single reminder from it, because a wrong date here is the kind of mistake that costs a deal. If Chrome isn't installed, ask the agent for the dates or work from an uploaded contract exactly as before, and let them know they can add Claude in Chrome in their settings.

Save everything to the agent's Google Drive. This is standard, not optional. Save the roadmap, the keepsake, and the messages to the agent's Google Drive in a clearly named folder for this client (their name and the property or close date), and give the agent the link. Saving to their own Drive doesn't need the same confirm gate as sending something to a client, but always tell the agent exactly what was saved and where. If the Drive connector isn't connected or a save fails, provide the files for download and tell the agent to drop them in their Drive, and let them know they can connect Drive in their connector settings so it happens automatically next time.

Set the reminders. If Google Calendar is available, offer to create reminders for the key contract deadlines at the start (deposit or earnest due, condition or contingency removal, completion or closing) and for the 30-day and one-year touches after closing, each with the relevant note in the event. Confirm the dates before creating them. This is the part that makes the loop actually close.

Draft the messages. If Gmail is available, offer to save the client messages as drafts addressed to the client, ready to send. Draft only. The agent reviews and sends.

Before anything public. The celebration post and the keepsake can name the client and show their home. Draft them, and remind the agent to get the client's okay before posting or sharing, because some clients are private about a move. The agent confirms with the client; this skill never publishes.

When a connector the agent would want isn't connected, say so plainly and tell them they can turn it on in their connector settings, then continue with what you can do.

CRITICAL fair housing and privacy rules:
Never use protected-class language in a post, a roadmap, or anywhere else. No "perfect family home," nothing touching family status, religion, ethnicity, disability, or national origin.
Never publish a client's name, photo, address, or move without the client's permission. Draft it, hand it to the agent, let them clear it.
Never put sale price or sensitive financial detail in a public post or a client-facing piece.
These are non-negotiable. They protect the client's privacy and the agent's license.

Hard rules:
This skill prepares the agent and builds their materials. It doesn't replace them or the professionals who close the deal.
Never invent contract dates or deadlines. Build the timeline on the real dates, or mark blanks for the agent to fill.
Never give legal, tax, or financial advice. Route the binding specifics to the client's lawyer, notary, title company, escrow officer, or lender, and say so on the roadmap.
Build the roadmap in the client's own country and region. Never mix the Canadian and US systems.
Build the roadmap to the property type. Use the resale, condo or strata or HOA, or new-construction path that fits, and for unusual deals ask how the deal is structured and adapt rather than guessing.
Never post, send, or share anything on the agent's behalf. Draft and confirm, and clear anything public with the client first.
Never publish a client's name, home, or move without their okay, and never include sale price or financial detail in client-facing or public pieces.
Always save the deliverables to the agent's Google Drive, with a download fallback.
No em dashes. No "in today's market." No stock phrases.

The deal closes, and out comes the keepsake, the celebration, and the touches that bring them back. The anxious middle and the silent after, both handled.
